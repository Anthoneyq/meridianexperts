Practice Areas

What we investigate.

Every engagement begins with the building — and ends with a record clear enough to stand on its own in deposition, mediation, or trial. Six disciplines, one standard of defensibility.

Practice 01

Construction Defects

Design and workmanship failures across commercial, multi-family, institutional, and custom residential construction. We investigate deviations from plans and specifications, failures to meet the applicable standard of care, and latent defects that emerge years after substantial completion.

Our work pairs document-first review with targeted field investigation — destructive and non-destructive — so that opinions on causation, responsibility, and cost of repair trace cleanly back to physical evidence.

  • Plan & Spec Review
  • Workmanship Analysis
  • Scope Disputes
  • Latent Defects
  • Cost of Repair
Practice 02

Water Intrusion & Envelope

Roofing, stucco, EIFS, window and door installations, flashing assemblies, masonry, and moisture migration pathways. We trace the route water took — not just the damage it left behind.

Where leaks are alleged, we conduct ASTM-referenced water testing, infrared thermography, and selective destructive openings to document the failure mode before opposing experts arrive on site.

  • ASTM E1105 Testing
  • EIFS & Stucco
  • Roofing Assemblies
  • Fenestration
  • Thermography
Practice 03

Insurance & Catastrophe Loss

Hurricane, hail, wind, and water-loss evaluations for first-party disputes, subrogation claims, and post-event loss analysis. Our reports isolate coverage-relevant causation — separating storm-caused damage from pre-existing conditions, wear, and maintenance-related loss.

We work both sides: retained by policyholder counsel and by insurer counsel, with the same evidentiary standard either way.

  • Hail & Wind
  • Hurricane
  • First-Party Disputes
  • Subrogation
  • Causation Analysis
Practice 04

Structural Failures

Settlement, differential movement, foundation distress, framing deficiencies, and load-path investigations evaluated against the building code and standard of care in effect at time of construction.

Engagements frequently involve coordination with licensed structural engineers, geotechnical consultants, and surveyors — all under a single investigative record prepared for disclosure.

  • Foundation & Settlement
  • Framing
  • Load Path
  • Geotechnical Coordination
Practice 05

Code & Standard of Care

Compliance analysis against governing codes (IBC, IRC, state and local amendments), published industry standards (ASTM, ASHRAE, NRCA, AAMA), and the prevailing standard of care — benchmarked to the date the work was performed, not today's standards.

We maintain historical code archives so that opinions on compliance are anchored to what was required at the time.

  • IBC / IRC
  • ASTM / ASHRAE
  • Historical Codes
  • Industry Standards
Practice 06

Expert Witness Testimony

Deposition and trial testimony supported by documented methodology, chain-of-custody evidence handling, and reports structured to separate observation, methodology, and opinion — the architecture a Daubert inquiry expects to see.

We prepare counsel for cross-examination of opposing experts and provide demonstrative exhibits built from our own investigative record.

  • Deposition
  • Trial Testimony
  • Daubert Readiness
  • Demonstratives
  • Cross Prep
Standards & Authority

The standards we cite.

Every opinion is benchmarked to a published standard of care — not a personal one. Below are the authorities most frequently referenced across our practice. Historical editions are maintained for engagements concerning work performed under prior versions of the code.

  • ASTM
    ASTM International
    Building performance, envelope, roofing, and materials standards — including E1105 water penetration testing and E06 committee work.
  • IBC / IRC
    International Building & Residential Codes
    Governing model codes, applied at the edition in effect on the date of construction — with state and local amendments layered in.
  • IIBEC
    Int'l Institute of Building Enclosure Consultants
    Envelope, roofing, and waterproofing consulting standards — including RBEC and RRO practice guidance.
  • AAMA / FGIA
    Fenestration & Glazing Industry Alliance
    Window, door, curtain-wall, and skylight installation, inspection, and performance standards.
  • ASCE / SEI
    American Society of Civil Engineers
    Structural loading (ASCE 7), existing-building evaluation (ASCE 41), and catastrophe-loss reconnaissance methodology.
  • NRCA
    National Roofing Contractors Association
    Roofing industry standards and technical guidance — commonly cited in hail, wind, and first-party roofing disputes.
  • ASHRAE
    Building Science & HVAC Performance
    Hygrothermal performance, vapor control, and ventilation standards — applied in moisture and IAQ-related matters.
  • NFPA
    National Fire Protection Association
    Fire-rated assemblies and life-safety provisions — applied to code-compliance and post-fire loss matters.
Questions From Counsel

What retaining counsel typically ask.

The questions below come up regularly during intake. If yours is different, send the case summary — we'll answer specifically in the fit assessment.

Do you accept both plaintiff and defense engagements?

Yes. We are retained by both plaintiff and defense counsel across construction-defect, insurance, and structural matters. The evidentiary standard we apply is the same either way — the opinion follows the record, not the retention.

How quickly can you engage on a matter with a near-term deadline?

Conflict check and intake review are completed within 24–48 hours of initial contact. For statute-of-limitations, pre-suit investigation, and inspection deadlines, we accommodate rush scheduling where we can staff the work without compromising documentation quality.

If we cannot meet the deadline with defensible work, we say so at intake — before any engagement letter is signed.

What information do you need at intake?

A one- or two-paragraph case summary, the procedural posture, the jurisdiction, key deadlines, and a list of parties for conflict check. Plans, prior reports, and photographs help, but are not required to start.

Privileged material should wait until an engagement letter is in place.

Do you decline engagements?

Yes — when the available evidence will not support a defensible opinion, when the scope requested sits outside our disciplines, or when a conflict exists. We communicate the decline in writing with a brief rationale so counsel can redirect without losing time.

How do you handle chain-of-custody for physical evidence?

All samples are labeled, photographed in-place prior to removal, logged into a chain-of-custody register, and stored under controlled conditions. Any transfer — to a laboratory, opposing expert, or storage facility — is signed at both ends. Our protocols track physical exhibits, photographs, measurements, and field logs.

Are your reports Rule 26 / Daubert compliant?

Reports are written to separate observation, methodology, and opinion, with basis disclosed under Rule 26. Methodology is grounded in published standards — ASTM, AAMA, IIBEC, IBC, and discipline-specific authorities — with citations embedded, not implied. The goal is a record that survives both Daubert inquiry and adversarial cross without restating the investigation from memory.

Where do you practice?

Primary jurisdictions are California, Arizona, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Colorado, Utah, and Texas. Additional jurisdictions are available under reciprocity or case-specific admission — we confirm jurisdictional fit at intake before engagement.

What is the typical engagement cost?

Scope and rate schedule are provided in writing at intake. Every engagement letter specifies hourly rates, anticipated phases, and the conditions under which scope may expand (e.g. additional destructive testing, rebuttal reports, depositions beyond the originally-noticed list). We scope to what the matter requires — not what runs up the invoice.
