Approach

Three layers. One defensible opinion.

Evidence, methodology, and opinion are separated by design — because that is the separation a court will eventually require.

A defensible opinion is not just a good opinion — it is a good opinion that can be explained, traced, and held up to adversarial scrutiny. Our approach is organized in three layers: evidence, methodology, and opinion. Each layer exists to protect the one above it.

Layer 01 · Foundation
Evidence

Physical observations, documents, and measurements — preserved under protocols that make them available to opposing experts on the same terms.

  • Chain-of-custody for samples
  • Dated, geotagged field photography
  • Destructive testing logs
  • Document and specification register
Layer 02 · Analysis
Methodology

The framework used to evaluate evidence against the applicable standard of care — explicitly cited, not assumed.

  • ASTM, ASCE, AAMA, and IIBEC standards
  • Building code at time of construction
  • Manufacturer installation specifications
  • Published failure-mode literature
Layer 03 · Conclusion
Opinion

Conclusions stated with the appropriate degree of certainty — and with the scope of each opinion explicitly bounded.

  • Separated from methodology in writing
  • Basis disclosed per Rule 26
  • Limitations acknowledged explicitly
  • Ready for deposition and cross
The reason a report survives Daubert is not the conclusion it reaches. It is the work that leads to the conclusion being visible on the page. — Placeholder quote, pending approval

What this means in practice

We decline engagements where the available evidence will not support a defensible opinion. We scope work to what the case actually requires — not what runs up the invoice. And we write reports so that a judge, a jury, or opposing counsel reading them for the first time can follow the reasoning end-to-end.

The result is not a louder opinion. It is a quieter one that holds.
