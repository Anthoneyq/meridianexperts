Why the structure of a report matters more than its length.

A Rule 26 report is not graded on weight. It is graded on whether observation, methodology, and opinion can be told apart at first reading — and whether each opinion traces to the specific evidence that supports it.

Published April 2026 Reading time 9 minutes Topic Methodology · Reporting

We read a lot of expert reports — ours, opposing experts', and occasionally reports produced by consulting experts who are unlikely to ever testify. A pattern holds across all three: the reports that survive cross-examination intact are not the longest, and not the most technically dense. They are the reports that, by page two, have already told the reader where observation ends, where methodology begins, and where an opinion is being offered.

That sounds basic. It is not. The majority of reports we see in intake blend all three into a single narrative voice, and opposing counsel will spend a deposition pulling the strands apart in front of the expert. The report should have done that work first.

The three-layer architecture

Every engagement we produce a written report on follows the same three-layer structure, visible on the page:

Layer 1 — Observation

What was observed, by whom, on what date, under what conditions, using what instrument, with what calibration record. The language is declarative. No inference is permitted in this section. If a moisture meter reads 24% at a given location, the report says so; it does not yet say that the reading indicates elevated moisture. That comes later.

Observations are keyed — every photograph, every measurement, every sample carries an identifier that every subsequent section will reference. A reader should be able to turn to any opinion in the report and, using the identifiers alone, reassemble the evidence on which the opinion rests. This is the audit trail. It is also what makes the report defensible when, two years later, the expert is asked to reconstruct the basis on a witness stand.

If an observation cannot be keyed to a specific photograph, log entry, or measurement, it does not belong in the report. It belongs in an interview note.

Layer 2 — Methodology

The standards, protocols, and analytical methods applied to the observations — cited to their published authority. ASTM E1105 water penetration testing. AAMA 502 field test for installed fenestration. IIBEC Manual of Practice procedures. Building code editions in effect on the date of construction. Academic references only where a published authority is controlling.

What does not appear in this layer: personal preference, proprietary methodology unavailable for review, and references to "common industry practice" without citation. An opposing expert will challenge any of the three under Daubert. The report should anticipate the challenge by naming the published authority and the edition — not by generalizing.

A useful test: if the methodology section were extracted and handed to a qualified expert in a different firm, that expert should be able to reproduce the analysis and arrive at a similar range of conclusions. If the method depends on judgment calls that are not articulated, the analysis is not reproducible, and the opinion is vulnerable.

Layer 3 — Opinion

Stated to a reasonable degree of professional certainty, each opinion cites (a) the observations on which it relies, by identifier, and (b) the methodology and standards under which those observations were evaluated. Nothing else.

Opinions are ranked. Where a cause is supported by multiple independent lines of evidence, the report says so, and says which lines. Where a cause is supported by a single line, the report says that too — and notes the conditions under which a reasonable expert might reach a different conclusion. Acknowledging the limits of the analysis is not weakness; it is the single most common factor in surviving cross.

Why the separation matters

Three reasons. All three come up in depositions we have watched go badly.

  1. Impeachment becomes narrow. When observation and opinion blur, opposing counsel can impeach the observation and use it to unwind the opinion. When the two are separated, impeaching an observation eliminates that specific supporting line — but the opinion, if supported by others, stands.
  2. Standard-of-care analysis becomes legible. Courts and juries handle a clear three-step structure far better than a flowing technical narrative. If the judge cannot follow how the expert got from "what was seen" to "what the standard required" to "what the opinion is," neither can the jury, and the report's weight diminishes regardless of its technical merit.
  3. Rebuttal is faster. When opposing counsel retains a rebuttal expert, a well-structured report narrows the ground for disagreement. The rebuttal expert must attack either an observation (factual), a method (standards-based), or an opinion (inferential) — and cannot pretend the attack is something it is not. This often shortens the rebuttal phase and narrows the contested issues at trial.

What this looks like in practice

In a water-intrusion matter we completed recently, the report ran 127 pages with approximately 900 photographs and test records. Opposing counsel's first question at the deposition of our expert was whether he could tie a specific leak location to a specific photograph and a specific test result. He could — by memory, because the photograph identifier, the test identifier, and the opinion identifier were printed alongside each other in the report. The deposition lasted one day rather than three. The matter settled within ninety days of the expert report exchange.

That is not a testimonial. It is an observation about what happens when the architecture of a report does its job before anyone is asked to explain it from the stand.
